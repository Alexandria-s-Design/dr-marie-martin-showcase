## SEGMENT 2: THE RESISTANCE (3:00 - 5:30)

**DR. MARTIN:**
"When I first introduced computational modeling to our science department, the reactions ranged from skepticism to outright resistance.

**[AUTHENTIC REENACTMENT - common objections]**

**Teacher objections:**
- 'My students can't handle this. It's too complex.'
- 'I don't have time. I'm barely getting through the required curriculum.'
- 'I'm not a computer science teacher. I teach biology.'
- 'This is just another fad that will be gone in two years.'
- 'We already have too many initiatives. Not one more thing.'

**[EMPATHETIC TONE]**

Here's what I want you to understand: **These objections weren't excuses. They were legitimate concerns.**

Teachers were:
- Overwhelmed with existing demands
- Understandably cautious about their own comfort with technology
- Protective of their students (especially in underserved communities)
- Burned out from years of 'innovative programs' that disappeared

So instead of dismissing these concerns, we addressed them head-on.

**Strategy 1: Start with the 'Why'**

Before any training on the 'how,' we had conversations about:
- Why computational thinking matters for students' futures
- How modeling builds deeper understanding than memorization
- What colleges and employers are looking for
- How this aligns with NGSS and our district goals

**Strategy 2: Identify Champions**

We didn't try to convert everyone at once. We found 2-3 teachers who were:
- Curious about innovation
- Willing to take risks
- Respected by their peers

These early adopters became our proof of concept.

**Strategy 3: Make It Easy**

We removed every possible barrier:
- Provided substitute coverage for planning time
- Created ready-to-use lesson plans
- Set up technology infrastructure in advance
- Offered one-on-one coaching (not just workshops)

**Lesson:** Change doesn't fail because teachers are resistant. It fails because leaders don't address the real barriers."

## SEGMENT 3: THE IMPLEMENTATION PROCESS (5:30 - 9:00)

**DR. MARTIN:**
"Once we had our champions identified, we followed a strategic rollout.

**Phase 1: Pilot with Early Adopters (Semester 1)**

**[GRAPHIC: Timeline showing phases]**

Two biology teachers volunteered to pilot ModelIt in one unit each:
- Teacher A: Cellular respiration and photosynthesis
- Teacher B: Infectious disease modeling (this was pre-COVID, ironically)

We provided:
- **3 hours of professional development** - hands-on modeling practice
- **Pre-built lesson sequences** - 3-week units ready to implement
- **Weekly check-ins** - troubleshooting and support
- **Classroom observations** - not evaluative, just supportive coaching

**Key Decision:** We didn't mandate assessment. We said, 'Try it, learn, and share your experience.' This reduced pressure and increased authentic feedback.

**Phase 2: Showcase Success (End of Semester 1)**

Our pilot teachers presented student work at a faculty meeting.

**[B-ROLL: Student-created models, student presentations]**

Students had created:
- Interactive models showing feedback loops in cellular processes
- Simulations of how diseases spread through populations
- Visual representations of complex systems they could manipulate and test

The faculty saw:
- **Student engagement** - kids excited about science
- **Depth of understanding** - students explaining complex concepts confidently
- **Equity** - all students, including English learners and struggling students, succeeding
- **Teacher confidence** - pilot teachers saying, 'If I can do this, anyone can'

**Phase 3: Expand the Circle (Semester 2)**

After the showcase, we had 5 more teachers volunteer.

This time:
- Pilot teachers co-facilitated the training
- We created a shared resource repository
- We established a monthly 'ModelIt learning community' meeting
- We celebrated small wins publicly (newsletters, social media, board presentations)

**Phase 4: Institutionalize (Year 2)**

By year two, computational modeling was:
- Written into our science curriculum maps
- Part of our new teacher onboarding
- Included in our budget for ongoing professional development
- Showcased at district and state conferences

**[GRAPHIC: Growth chart showing teacher and student participation]**

Year 1: 2 teachers, 120 students
Year 2: 8 teachers, 480 students
Year 3: All secondary science teachers, 1,200+ students

**Critical Success Factor: We never made it mandatory until we had critical mass of voluntary adoption.**

When 80% of your department is already doing it and loving it, the last 20% will naturally follow."

## SEGMENT 5: LESSONS LEARNED & ADVICE (11:00 - 13:30)

**DR. MARTIN:**
"If you're trying to implement innovation in your school, here's what I want you to take away:

**Lesson 1: Innovation Doesn't Scale Through Mandates**

Top-down mandates create compliance, not commitment.

Instead:
- Start small with volunteers
- Create proof of concept
- Let success spread organically
- Provide support, not pressure

**Lesson 2: Professional Development Is Ongoing, Not One-And-Done**

Our biggest mistake in year one? Thinking a 3-hour workshop was enough.

What actually worked:
- Monthly learning communities
- Classroom coaching
- Peer observations
- Shared planning time

**Lesson 3: Technology Is the Tool, Not the Goal**

ModelIt didn't work because it's cool technology. It worked because it enabled pedagogical practices we valued:
- Student-centered inquiry
- Authentic problem-solving
- Collaborative learning
- Deep understanding of complex systems

**Always ask:** How does this technology make better teaching and learning possible?

**Lesson 4: Address Teacher Mindset First**

Before we could change practice, we had to shift beliefs:
- From 'my students can't' to 'how can I support them to succeed?'
- From 'I'm not a tech person' to 'I'm a learner alongside my students'
- From 'this won't work here' to 'what would make this work here?'

**Lesson 5: Make It Easier Than the Status Quo**

Innovation fails when it adds work without removing work.

We made sure:
- Lesson plans were ready to use
- Technology setup was handled
- Assessment rubrics were provided
- Success didn't depend on extra hours from already-overworked teachers

**[GRAPHIC: Implementation Checklist]**

**Your Innovation Implementation Checklist:**
✓ Clear 'why' grounded in student outcomes
✓ Early adopters identified and supported
✓ Barriers removed (time, resources, training)
✓ Ongoing support structures established
✓ Success showcased and celebrated
✓ Scalable pathway defined
✓ Metrics for measuring impact identified"
